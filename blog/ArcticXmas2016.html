  3. Comments on the extreme December 2016 Arctic cyclone and its impact on the Barents-Kara seas.

Using NASA's Atmospheric Infrared Sounder (AIRS) instrument onboard NASA's Aqua satellite, part of the A-train constellation of satellites, we are able to study the recent "extreme" Arctic Winter Cyclone, following almost a year to-the-day after a very similar storm hit the Atlantic sector of the Arctic last December (2015).

That event, and its impact on the Barents-Kara sea ice was explored in our recent journal article published in Monthly Weather Review , and was the topic of a NASA press release and visualization.

Like the December 2015 winter storm, a very warm and humid air mass was transportde into the central Arctic region by this cyclone, causing ~4.5 C (1.5 standard deviations above the 2003-14 mean) warmer temperatures and 1 g/kg (>3 standard deviations above the 2003-14 mean) wetter conditions between December 21-22, 2016 in the Kara and Barents seas region.

The storm also carried in very warm temperatures into the area surrounding the North Pole, with average daily temperatures reaching +0.85 C on December 22, 2016. This is very rare, as the North Pole does not see temperatures above freezing in the winter months. Specific humidity at the North Pole reached 3.9 g/kg, around 3 g/kg wetter than normal conditions in winter.

This storm, although very comparable in magnitude to the December 2015 storm had less of an impact on the Kara Barents seas region, This is most likely due to the difference in storm tracks between the two storms.

Nonetheless, this 2016 winter storm, wreaked havoc on the more vulnerable Arctic sea ice pack (alek perhaps add a figure of the ice con??), buy pushing the sea ice pack farther North. With this same magnitude storm occurring the past two Decembers NASA scientists wonder if this is the "new normal" for the Arctic in winter.
